# Kubernetes Kingdom — Campaign Review

> **Purpose:** Professional post-design review of the Kubernetes Kingdom campaign across 12 dimensions.
> **Status:** v1 — review only. Do not modify the campaign based on this document without a separate approval step.
> **Owned by:** AI
> **Scope:** All 14 stages (13 mandatory + Final), 3 expandable regions, 5 recurring cast members.

---

## Executive Summary

1. **The campaign's core premise is strong and coherent.** Concept-as-wound is a genuine design innovation that holds across all 14 stages. The campaign earns its premise.
2. **Act 2 (Stages 5–9) is the campaign's highest risk zone.** Five stages in sequence, each introducing a new operational concept, with tighter emotional differentiation between stages than Act 1 or Act 3. ConfigMaps and Secrets back-to-back at the same density is the single most likely player fatigue point.
3. **The boss progression has a structural gap at Acts 1–2.** Stages 1–3 bosses and Stages 5–8 bosses test single-stage concepts. The jump to the Isolation Wyrm (4-concept cross-test) and the Severed Envoy (5-concept consequential chain) may feel abrupt without intermediate cross-concept preparation.
4. **Voss's arc is deliberately unresolved — but needs one more observable beat in Act 3 to sustain interest through the Final Stage.** Currently his Stage 13 appearance is passive. A player who has been waiting for Voss to resolve for five stages may feel let down.
5. **The Final Stage is the campaign's strongest design.** Understanding → Disagreement → Confrontation → Restoration is a genuinely rare climax structure for an educational game. It should be protected from scope creep.
6. **Campaign length (14 stages) is at the upper edge of what most players will complete.** The distribution across acts (4 / 5 / 4 / 1) is defensible, but Act 2's density creates a real drop-off risk around Stages 7–8.
7. **The expandable regions are underspecified.** E3 (Chaos Cluster) in particular has no anchor NPC or emotional hook — it risks feeling like a bonus dungeon rather than optional depth.
8. **Mira's arc is the campaign's most reliable emotional anchor.** Protect it. She appears too rarely in Act 3 (only Stage 10 before the epilogue).

## Dimension 1 — Story Progression

### Finding 1.1 — The kingdom's wound is established well but the dragon's strategy is revealed too late
**Priority: Significant**

The campaign establishes Khaosynth's motivation clearly in the cast section and the Final Stage, but the player has no access to his logic until Stage 13 (the intercept). For 12 stages, the dragon is felt as presence and damage, not as a coherent agent with a philosophy. This is largely intentional — and it works — but the player has no way to understand why the damage follows the specific pattern it does (base layer first, operational layer second, deep layer last) until Act 3.

**Why it matters:** A player who feels the damage as random rather than strategic will not appreciate the elegance of Khaosynth's plan. The "he let me rebuild — he was waiting" payoff in Act 2's portal transition is the campaign's best structural beat, but it only lands fully if the player senses earlier that the dragon is deliberate, not chaotic.

**Proposed solution:** Plant 1–2 earlier environmental signals of Khaosynth's deliberateness — not exposition, but observable pattern. Example: in Stage 3 (Deployments), Kestran notices that the Mandate Breakers attacked the records before the Pods — a specific order that suggests intent, not random corruption. A player who notices this pattern will experience Stage 13's intercept as confirmation, not revelation.

---

### Finding 1.2 — Act 2's portal transitions are weaker than Act 1's
**Priority: Minor**

Act 1's portal transitions are emotionally specific: the Fields reassembling, Jonn finally delivering his message, Kestran's first direct order. Act 2's transitions trend toward Lyra making an observation and pointing the player at the next region. Stages 5, 7, and 8 portal transitions are functionally the same beat: Lyra explains what comes next.

**Why it matters:** Portal transitions are the player's session boundary and emotional marker for "I completed something." If they feel formulaic, stage completion loses its emotional punctuation.

**Proposed solution:** Give each portal transition in Act 2 a human moment that doesn't belong to Lyra. Stage 7: Thresh acknowledging that specific recovered data is from someone he knew personally. Stage 8: Kestran's "So the kingdom had a name for it" is already there — make the portal transition *his* moment, not Lyra's observation about the next region.

## Dimension 2 — Character Arcs

### Finding 2.1 — Kestran's "why he stayed" revelation is promised but not delivered
**Priority: Significant**

The cast section states: *"Act 3 — reveals why he stayed when others fled (he was born in this kingdom and he will die defending it)."* This revelation is mentioned in the cast overview but does not appear in any specific stage entry in Act 3. Stage 13 has his most significant moment (*"When you're ready"*) but the stated revelation — why he stayed — is absent from the designed stage beats.

**Why it matters:** If this revelation exists only in the cast section and never lands in a specific stage, it won't exist in the game. Players will not experience it.

**Proposed solution:** Assign the revelation to Stage 12 (Monitoring). The restored Watchtower shows Kestran things he couldn't see from the ground. In that moment of expanded visibility — seeing the kingdom whole — he says something brief about why he never left. It does not need to be long. It needs to be present in a specific stage.

---

### Finding 2.2 — Voss's Stage 13 appearance is too passive for the investment the player has made
**Priority: Significant**

Voss appears in Stages 6, 7, 9, 10 (per cast section), and 13. By Stage 13 the player has spent five stages wondering about him. His appearance in Stage 13 is: he doesn't enter the deep chambers, he says nothing when the intercept plays, and he says nothing about what he may have heard. This is intentional ambiguity — but it risks reading as the design running out of things to do with him rather than a deliberate choice.

**Why it matters:** Deliberate unresolution is only satisfying if the player believes the writer knew the answer and chose not to give it. Passive presence in the campaign's most dramatic stage can read as the writer not knowing either.

**Proposed solution:** Give Voss one specific action in Stage 13 that is observable and interpretable in two directions. Example: he is found not in the outer sections but in the deep chambers — having already been there when the player arrives, as designed — but specifically he is standing at the point where the intercept channel is located. Whether he was listening, waiting, or placed there by Khaosynth is unanswerable. But it is specific, and it is charged.

---

### Finding 2.3 — Mira disappears from Act 3 after Stage 10
**Priority: Minor**

Mira appears in Stages 2, 5, 10, and the Final epilogue. In Act 3, she appears in Stage 10 and then is absent from Stages 11, 12, and 13 before reappearing in the epilogue. Four stages is a long absence for the campaign's primary emotional mirror.

**Why it matters:** The epilogue's payoff — Mira at the Watchtower, working, no longer waiting — depends on the player still caring about her. A four-stage absence before the epilogue is a relationship that has gone cold.

**Proposed solution:** A single line of presence in Stage 12 (the Watchtower) is already there in the design. The stage says she *"points to the Hollow Fields — Stage 1 — and says she remembers the day the player arrived."* This is strong. Ensure it is marked as a required beat, not optional flavour.

---

### Finding 2.4 — Lyra's "decision-maker frontier" in Act 3 is described but not demonstrated
**Priority: Significant**

The Act Transition document states: *"The deep kingdom's corruption requires judgement — knowing which systems to trust, which to destroy, which to leave ambiguous. This is the frontier of her growth: from information-gatherer to decision-maker. She is not there yet. Act 3 is where she reaches."*

None of the Act 3 stage entries show Lyra making a decision rather than a recommendation. Stage 11 (she asks the player to teach her) and Stage 13 (she immediately starts auditing) are good beats — but neither shows her making a judgement call the player does not know how to validate.

**Why it matters:** An arc that is described in a transition document but not demonstrated in stage design does not exist in the game.

**Proposed solution:** In Stage 13, Lyra identifies a system that looks corrupted but cannot be confirmed — it might be Khaosynth's, it might be the kingdom's own. She makes a call: preserve it or purge it. She is not certain. The player sees her make an irreversible decision without a right answer. This is the frontier. One moment. Stage 13 can hold it.

## Dimension 3 — Learning Progression

### Finding 3.1 — The concept sequence is defensible and well-ordered
**Priority: None (strength)**

Containers → Pods → Deployments → Services → ConfigMaps → Secrets → Volumes → Scheduling → Networking → Ingress → Helm → Monitoring → Security → Troubleshooting follows genuine Kubernetes learning prerequisites. The pairing of ConfigMaps and Secrets (density 5/5, back-to-back) is the tightest cluster, but is justified by their conceptual adjacency and differentiated by emotional register.

---

### Finding 3.2 — Troubleshooting as a synthesis concept in the Final Stage is correct but needs a concrete knowledge anchor
**Priority: Significant**

The Final Stage correctly identifies Troubleshooting as synthesis rather than a new concept. However, the stage design does not specify what the player is actually doing when they "diagnose" their way through Khaosynth's cluster. The movement through Khaosynth's cluster is described narratively but not as specific learning activities.

**Why it matters:** Troubleshooting without a concrete methodology risks feeling like "explore and click things." The player needs a framework — even a simple one — to feel like they are troubleshooting rather than wandering.

**Proposed solution:** Define 3–4 explicit troubleshooting actions the player performs in Movement 2 (Disagreement): for example, comparing desired state to actual state (Deployments knowledge), checking whether the correct credentials are mounted (Secrets knowledge), verifying that a workload is on the correct Node (Scheduling knowledge), reading a monitoring signal to identify a degrading component (Monitoring knowledge). These are the diagnosis steps. They are drawn from prior stages. They make the synthesis concrete.

## Dimension 4 — Concept Pacing

### Finding 4.1 — Stages 5 and 6 risk blurring despite differentiated emotional registers
**Priority: Significant**

ConfigMaps and Secrets are adjacent in concept and adjacent in the campaign. Both are about data a Pod reads at runtime. Both use the same consumption mechanisms (env vars, mounted files). The emotional registers are differentiated (grief for forgotten knowledge vs. responsibility for vulnerable knowledge) — but a player who misses or skims the story beats will experience them as two stages about "runtime configuration."

**Why it matters:** If a player cannot distinguish what they learned in Stage 5 from what they learned in Stage 6, one of them didn't land.

**Proposed solution:** Ensure the observable entry beat for each stage makes the distinction unmissable before any concept name appears. Stage 5's opening is systems running wrong (observable misconfiguration). Stage 6's opening needs to be systems appearing right but having been exposed (invisible breach, discovered consequence) — not just a locked door. The feeling of Stage 5 is "something was forgotten"; the feeling of Stage 6 must be "something that should have been private was read."

---

### Finding 4.2 — Act 3 concept pacing is appropriately lighter, but Stage 10's deferred callback needs explicit design
**Priority: Minor**

Stage 9's deferred discovery (too-restrictive vs. too-permissive NetworkPolicy) is assigned to Stage 10 as an "Act 3 callback." The Stage 10 design references this callback in the density note but does not specify *how* the callback is delivered — as a scroll, NPC dialogue, dungeon beat, or quest reveal.

**Why it matters:** Deferred concepts that are not explicitly designed tend to get dropped.

**Proposed solution:** Assign the callback to a specific delivery mechanism in Stage 10. Recommended: the Sealed Sovereign boss's behaviour (overly-restrictive attacks that let through nothing; or overly-permissive phases that let through everything) is the concrete demonstration of both extremes in a single fight. The callback is in the boss, not in a separate discovery beat.

**Proposed solution:** Retroactively assign density estimates to Stages 1–4. Estimated: Stage 1 (4), Stage 2 (4), Stage 3 (5), Stage 4 (5). Log these in the campaign document.

## Dimension 6 — Quest Progression

### Finding 6.1 — Quest structure is described at stage level but not at campaign level
**Priority: Significant**

Each stage specifies that quests exist and describes their general type. But there is no campaign-level view of how quests escalate: how many quests per stage, whether quests become multi-step across acts, whether Act 3 quests are structurally different from Act 1 quests.

**Why it matters:** Without campaign-level quest progression, all quests risk feeling like the same pattern at different concept levels. "Talk to NPC → learn concept → defeat enemy → return" is the explicit example in prompt2.md and likely the default unless escalation is explicitly designed.

**Proposed solution:** This is appropriately out of scope for the campaign design document and belongs in `ai-quest-system.md`. Flag it as a required output for that doc: define at least three quest archetypes (introduction quest, application quest, synthesis quest) and specify which archetype dominates each act.

## Dimension 7 — Boss Progression

### Finding 7.1 — Single-concept bosses in Acts 1–2 do not prepare the player for cross-concept act bosses
**Priority: Critical**

Stages 1–3 bosses and Stages 5–8 bosses each test a single stage's concept. The Isolation Wyrm (Act 1 boss) suddenly requires the player to demonstrate all four Act 1 concepts across four phases. The Severed Envoy (Act 2 boss) requires five Act 2 concepts in a consequential chain. There is no graduated preparation for this structural shift — no boss between Stage 3 and Stage 4 that tests two concepts, no boss between Stage 8 and Stage 9 that combines two adjacent Act 2 concepts.

**Why it matters:** The jump from single-concept to four-concept boss within the same act (Stage 3 boss tests Deployments; Stage 4 boss tests all four Act 1 concepts) is the campaign's largest single difficulty spike. A player who has never been asked to combine concepts mid-fight will be surprised.

**Proposed solution:** One option per act: in Stage 4 (Services), reframe the Isolation Wyrm to have a brief Stage 1–3 warmup phase before the full four-phase structure — signalling "this fight is different" through its first exchange before demanding full cross-concept knowledge. Alternatively, seed cross-concept mini-challenges into Stage 3's dungeon (a mini-challenge that requires both Deployment and Pod knowledge) so the player has practiced the combination before the act boss tests it.

---

### Finding 7.2 — The Corrupted Warden (Act 3 boss) stands down when given the right answer — this must be clearly communicated
**Priority: Significant**

The Corrupted Warden's resolution — it accepts a correctly assembled RBAC rule set and stands down — is the campaign's most conceptually interesting boss moment. It also requires the player to understand that they can win by *presenting the correct answer*, not just by dealing damage. If this mechanic is not clearly telegraphed in the dungeon preceding the boss, players will not know it is available.

**Why it matters:** A player who spends the fight trying to out-damage the Warden and fails repeatedly will not discover the resolution mechanic through trial and error — they will quit.

**Proposed solution:** The mini-boss in the dungeon (the Privilege Escalator) should be defeated by the same mechanic in miniature: present minimum-necessary permissions and it de-escalates. This seeds the pattern before the boss fight requires it at scale.

---

## Dimension 8 — Emotional Progression

### Finding 8.1 — Empathy peaks in Act 1 and is not reinforced adequately in Act 2
**Priority: Significant**

Empathy (target emotion §3.4) is most strongly designed in Act 1: Mira's broken home in Stage 2, Jonn's message in Stage 4, the village beginning to reconnect. Act 2 NPCs (Hadris, Cassel, Thresh, Orthen, Nett) are technically competent and well-characterised, but they are professionals at work rather than people with personal stakes visible to the player. The emotional register of Act 2 is responsibility and competence — but not empathy.

**Why it matters:** Empathy for the kingdom's people is what makes Act 3's "defend what you restored" feel like defending people, not systems. If Act 2 doesn't replenish empathy, Act 3 opens with a deficit.

**Proposed solution:** Give one Act 2 NPC a personal stake that is revealed late in their stage — not as a quest hook, but as a human detail. Example: Thresh in Stage 7 mentions that the specific Volume data he most wants to recover belonged to someone who didn't survive the attack. This costs nothing in stage design and pays dividends in Act 3.

---

### Finding 8.2 — Wonder is designed for but not explicitly tracked
**Priority: Minor**

Wonder (target emotion §3.2) is addressed through the pacing rule "every screen has something worth approaching" and through stage descriptions like Stage 9's "the most beautiful region." But no stage entry explicitly tracks or designs for wonder as a distinct beat.

**Why it matters:** Wonder is the emotion most easily lost to familiarity — if every stage feels visually similar, it disappears. Without explicit design attention, it becomes implicit set dressing.

**Proposed solution:** Each stage should have one designed wonder beat in the design document: something the player will see or encounter that is worth pausing for independent of its quest or knowledge function. Stage 9 (the Web, "vast luminous lattice") already does this well. Apply the same explicitness to Stages 5, 6, and 7 which describe functional environments without a designed wow-moment.

## Dimension 9 — Repetition Risks

### Finding 9.1 — The 9-beat stage arc is the campaign's primary repetition risk
**Priority: Critical**

Every mandatory stage follows: Arrival → Exploration → Discovery → Quests → Encounters → Mini-challenges → Dungeon → Boss → Portal. Across 13 mandatory stages, this arc repeats 13 times. The arc is sound structurally — but unless the content of each beat is visually, narratively, and mechanically distinct, players will feel the structure by Stage 6 and be managing it rather than experiencing it by Stage 10.

**Why it matters:** A player who is aware of the stage structure is already partially outside the game. Structural awareness is the enemy of immersion.

**Proposed solution:** Three approaches that do not require changing the arc:
1. **Vary the entry.** Not every stage should begin with Arrival → Exploration in the same order. Stage 6 (Secrets) could begin with the discovery of a breach before the player has explored the region. Stage 9 (Networking) could begin with the player *in* the Web — already inside, disoriented — rather than approaching it.
2. **Vary the dungeon's relationship to the concept.** Act 1 dungeons apply the stage's concept. Act 2 dungeons should increasingly combine the current concept with earlier ones. Stage 8's dungeon is already doing this (node affinity + taints/tolerations). Make this deliberate and escalating.
3. **Let one stage in each act skip a beat.** A stage that has no dungeon, or no formal boss (replaced by a different kind of confrontation), signals that the arc is a tendency, not a law.

---

### Finding 9.2 — Enemy type naming follows a predictable pattern
**Priority: Minor**

Enemy naming across the campaign: Shell Beetles, Image Wraiths, Pod Bugs, Orphan Shades, Mandate Breakers, Rollback Wraiths, Directory Eaters, Misdirection Sprites, Parameter Leeches, Default Haunts, Credential Feeders, Exposure Wraiths, Flood Wraiths, Void Seekers, Misplacement Wraiths, Resource Drains, Packet Feeders, Route Corruptors, Policy Ghosts, Gate Corruptors, Seal Wraiths, Value Corruptors, Release Wraiths, Noise Feeders, Silence Wraiths, Permission Feeders. The pattern "[Concept word] + [generic creature type]" is predictable enough to read as a template by Act 2.

**Why it matters:** Enemy naming is part of world texture. Predictable names reduce the sense of a living world.

**Proposed solution:** Not a priority for campaign design — belongs in content authoring. Flag for the asset and content phase: introduce 3–4 enemy names in Act 2 and Act 3 that do not follow the pattern and feel native to their region's lore rather than their concept's vocabulary.

## Dimension 10 — Drop-off Risks

### Finding 10.1 — Stage 7 (Volumes) is the campaign's highest drop-off risk
**Priority: Critical**

Stage 7 sits at the midpoint of Act 2 (Stage 5 of 9), introduces a concept (Volumes) that is less immediately intuitive than ConfigMaps or Secrets, is the first stage to go underground (new visual register), and its NPC Brix is the only comic-relief-adjacent character in the campaign ("cheerfully oblivious to conceptual weight"). The combination of mid-act placement, conceptual difficulty, visual shift, and tonal change creates the highest single drop-off risk in the campaign.

**Why it matters:** If a player quits at Stage 7, they have learned Containers, Pods, Deployments, Services, ConfigMaps, and Secrets — but not Volumes, Scheduling, Networking, or anything in Act 3. That is a significant learning gap.

**Proposed solution:**
1. Give Stage 7 a stronger emotional opening than the current "scribe's records gone after restart" — while that is good, it is quiet. Consider opening with something the player has a personal stake in: records from a character they met in Act 1 (Bram's crop rotation records, Sera's village census) that were lost. The player's existing relationship makes the loss land harder.
2. Consider whether Brix's "cheerful obliviousness" is the right tone for Stage 7. He may read as comic relief in a stage that needs weight. His personality is described as "cheerfully oblivious to the conceptual weight of what they are doing" — but the conceptual weight is the point of this stage. Consider making Brix professional-focused-on-the-physical without the comedic framing.

---

### Finding 10.2 — The Final Stage risks pacing fatigue after Stage 13's Corrupted Warden boss
**Priority: Significant**

Stage 13 ends with the Corrupted Warden (Act 3 boss) — a multi-phase, multi-layer boss fight that is explicitly the campaign's most conceptually demanding regular boss. Immediately after, the player enters Stage 14 (the Dragon's Throne). The Final Stage begins with a slow, narrative-heavy movement (Understanding) before reaching the Confrontation. A player who is boss-fatigued from Stage 13 may disengage during Stage 14's first two movements.

**Why it matters:** The Final Stage is the campaign's most important stage. If the player arrives depleted, the emotional payoff is diminished.

**Proposed solution:** Give the player a deliberate rest beat between Stage 13's portal transition and Stage 14's opening. The portal transition already has Kestran's *"When you're ready"* — but the stage design should make explicit that the player can return to restored regions, speak to characters, or simply exist in the world before committing to Stage 14. This is not a mechanical save point; it is a designed breathing space. The Final Stage should open only when the player chooses to proceed through Kestran's gate.

---

### Finding 10.3 — Act 2 as a whole is a sustained drop-off risk
**Priority: Significant**

Act 2 contains five stages, each introducing a new concept, at knowledge densities of 5/5/6/6/6. The act's emotional registers are responsibility and competence — valid, important — but neither is as immediately gripping as Act 1's wonder-and-discovery or Act 3's mastery-and-stakes. A player who does not have strong intrinsic motivation for Kubernetes content may not feel sufficient narrative pull through five consecutive operational-concept stages.

**Why it matters:** Act 2 is where the campaign teaches the most essential operational knowledge. Losing players here means losing the campaign's educational core.

**Proposed solution:** Strengthen the Voss thread through Act 2. His appearances in Stages 6, 7, and 9 are currently episodic — he provides information and disappears. Making his presence more active (he is *following* the player between stages, observed in Stage 8's environment even without a formal appearance) would create a narrative thread that runs through all of Act 2 and gives players a reason to continue beyond the concept-of-the-week.

## Dimension 11 — Player Confusion Risks

### Finding 11.1 — The Isolated Wyrm boss's four-phase structure needs clear phase signposting
**Priority: Significant**

The Isolation Wyrm tests all four Act 1 concepts across four phases. A player who does not understand *which concept is being tested in each phase* will not know what to do when they fail. Without clear phase-concept signposting, failed phases will feel arbitrary.

**Why it matters:** If failure in a multi-phase boss feels arbitrary, it is punishing. Pillar 3 (failure teaches, never punishes) requires that failure reveals what the player got wrong — which requires the player to know which concept they were being tested on.

**Proposed solution:** Each phase of the Isolation Wyrm should have an observable signal that identifies the concept being tested before the challenge appears. The signal does not need to be a title card — it can be environmental. Phase 2 (Pods): the boss's attack pattern isolates a section of the battlefield that behaves like a Pod grouping breaking apart. The player who recognises the pattern knows what's coming.

---

### Finding 11.2 — Khaosynth's "he let me rebuild — he was waiting" beat depends on the player understanding his strategy
**Priority: Minor**

The Act 2 portal transition in Stage 9 states: *"A kingdom that is stable is a kingdom he can no longer ignore. In Act 1 he left it fragmented and trusted it would collapse. In Act 2, the player proved it would not. Act 3 is his response."* This is a narrative explanation in the design document. It needs a corresponding in-world beat — something the player observes or hears — that makes Khaosynth's strategic patience legible without explaining it in text.

**Why it matters:** The strategic patience beat is one of the campaign's best narrative ideas. If it only exists in the design document, it doesn't exist in the game.

**Proposed solution:** Voss is the correct delivery vehicle. In Stage 9's portal transition, Voss says something that implies the dragon expected the restoration to happen — and expected to act after it did. He does not explain why this is significant. The player connects the dots.

---

## Dimension 12 — Campaign Length Risks

### Finding 12.1 — 14 stages is long for an educational RPG; distribution is defensible but act lengths are uneven
**Priority: Significant**

Act 1: 4 stages. Act 2: 5 stages. Act 3: 4 stages. Final: 1 stage.

Act 2 at 5 stages is the longest act. This is the most conceptually dense act and the one with the highest drop-off risk. The longest act being the hardest act is a structural risk.

**Why it matters:** A player who reaches the midpoint of the campaign (Stage 7, the campaign's established highest drop-off point) has completed only 50% of Act 2 and 50% of the campaign. If they quit at Stage 7 feeling that the campaign has a lot more of the same ahead, the structure has not helped them.

**Proposed solution:** Consider rebalancing to Act 1: 4, Act 2: 4, Act 3: 5, Final: 1 — which puts the most material in the act with the highest emotional momentum (Act 3). This would require moving one Act 2 concept to Act 3. Ingress (Stage 10) is already the Act 3 opener and conceptually bridges networking (Act 2) and security/deep systems (Act 3). Moving Networking (Stage 9) to Act 3 and ending Act 2 at Scheduling is one option — but this has concept-prerequisite implications that need evaluation. **Flag for design review before acting on.**

---

### Finding 12.2 — The three expandable regions have no specified player level ranges or placement signals
**Priority: Minor**

E1, E2, E3 are described by concept and general idea but not by: when they unlock, what signals their availability to the player, what their relative difficulty is, or what emotional hook draws the player in.

**Why it matters:** Optional content that players do not know exists is not optional — it is missing.

**Proposed solution:** Add to each expandable region: unlock condition (which mandatory stage completion unlocks it), discovery method (how the player learns it exists), and emotional hook (why a player would want to explore it beyond "more content"). E3 (Chaos Cluster) particularly needs an anchor NPC who has a reason to draw the player there — without one, it is just a harder zone.

## What Is Working Well

1. **Concept-as-wound is a genuine design innovation.** Every region's damage maps to its concept in a way that is felt before it is named. This principle holds across all 14 stages.
2. **Khaosynth's motivation is thematically coherent and original.** A villain who is wrong about what to do about failure — not a villain who is evil — is rare in educational games and earns the campaign its finale.
3. **The Severed Envoy's four-phase consequential chain is the campaign's strongest boss design.** Each phase exposes the next; the fight has a logic; the player solves one problem, not four.
4. **The Final Stage's four-movement structure (Understanding → Disagreement → Confrontation → Restoration) is an appropriate climax for an educational RPG.** Protecting it from scope creep is a design priority.
5. **Kestran's arc is earned.** The seeding in Stage 3, the recognition in Stage 8, the *"When you're ready"* in Stage 13 — this arc does not require exposition because it is shown in behaviour.
6. **The deferred NetworkPolicy discovery (Stage 9 → Stage 10) is well-motivated.** Moving the highest-complexity discovery out of Act 2's most demanding stage is a good pacing call.
7. **The emotional differentiation between ConfigMaps (grief for forgotten knowledge) and Secrets (responsibility for vulnerable knowledge) is well-designed** — provided the entry beats deliver the feeling before the concept.
8. **Mira's epilogue is the correct emotional fullstop.** The player's arc ends; hers continues. This is rare and right.
